The importance of process control

Process:
Refers to the methods of changing or refining raw materials to create end products.

The raw materials (which pass through or remain in liquid, gas or solids states) during the process can be :
- Transferred , Measured , Mixed , Heated Or Cooled ,
- Filtered , Stored , Handled in other way
- To produce the end-product.

Process Industries include:
Chemical industry , oil and gas industry , Food industries , water treatment industry , power industry ,…….

Process control:
Refers to the methods that are used to control process variables when manufacturing a product.

**Examples:
- Control proportion of one ingredient to another
- Control temperature or pressure of material
- Control mixing of ingredients


Process control technology is the tool that enables manufacturers to keep their operations running within specified limits and to set more precise limits to maximize profitability, ensure quality and safety

The importance of process control

The importance of process control:
- Reduce variability
- Increase efficiency
- Ensure safety

A. Reduce variability
Manufactures can save money by reducing variability.

Example:
In gasoline blending product, as many as 12 or more different components may be blended to make a specific grade of gasoline.
If the refinery does not have precise control over the flow of the separate components , the gasoline may get too much of the high –octane components.

As a result, customers would receive a high-grade gasoline than they paid for, and the refinery would lose money.
And the opposite situation could also happen.


A. Reduce variability
Reducing variability reduce the need of product padding.

Padding:
The process of making a product of higher quality than it needs to meet the specifications.

Where manufacturers are forced to pad the product when the process control is poor and there is variability in the end product to ensure that specifications are met.

So, with accurate process control, the optimal point can be moved to be closer to the actual product specification and thus save the manufacturer money.

The importance of process control

B. Increase Efficiency
Some processes need to be maintained at a specific point to maximize the efficiency.

** Examples:
- A control point might be the temperature at which a chemical reaction takes place.
- Control the level of water to be at the mid of the boiler to have the maximum boiler efficiency.

Manufacturers save money by minimizing the resources required to produce the end-product.

C. Ensure Safety
Precise control is required to ensure safety.

** Examples:
- Maintaining proper boiler pressure by controlling the inflow of air used in combustion and the outflow of exhaust gases

A run away process (such as an out of control nuclear or chemical reaction ) shall be controlled as the consequence of a run away process can be catastrophic.

The term automatic process control came into wide use when people learned to adapt automatic regulatory procedures to manufacturer products or process material more efficiently.

Such procedure are called automatic as no manual intervention is required to regulate them

Control theory basics


The control loop
Control loops in the process control industry require three tasks to occur:

- Measure

Control theory basics

Range
Defined in IEC 60902 as follows: The region of values between the lower and upper limits of the quantity under consideration. It is expressed by stating the lower and upper limits (e.g. minus 1 to 10 bar (a)).

Span
Defined in IEC 60902 as follows: The algebraic difference between the upper and lower limit values of a given range. It is expressed as a figure and unit of measurement (e.g. 11 bar).

Control theory basics


LRL
Lower Range Limit; the lowest quantity that a device is designed to measure.

URL
Upper Range Limit; the highest quantity that a device is designed to measure.

Instrument range
The region in which the instrument is designed to operate. It is a physical capability of the device. The region limits are expressed by stating the LRL and URL.


Instrument minimum span
The minimum distance between the URV and LRV for which the instrument is designed. It is a physical limitation of the device.

Instrument maximum span
The maximum distance between the URV and LRV for which the instrument is designed. It is a physical limitation of the device.

Process control terms


Process Variable (PV)
Is a condition of the process fluid (liquid or gas) that can change the manufacturing process.

** In the example of the tank , the process variable is level.
** Common process variables include:
- Pressure
- Level
- Flow
- Temperature
- Density
- Liquid interface
- PH
- Mass
- Conductivity

Process control terms

Setpoint (SP)
Is a value for the process parameter that is desired to be maintained.

Measured variable or Controlled variable (Y / MV)
Is a condition of the process fluid (liquid or gas) that is required to be kept as the designated setpoint.

Manipulated Variable (U)
Is the variable controlled by process Controller in order to offset the Process Disturbance. The variables Y and U are related to each other; hence a variation in U can cause a variation in Y

Sometimes the measured variable is not same as the process variable.

** Example:
Measure flow into and out of a storage tank to determine the tank level.

Process control terms


Closed control loop
A closed control loop exists where a process variable is measured , compared to a setpoint and action is taken to correct any deviation from set point.

Process control terms

Feedback control loop

Feedback control loop


The controlled variable (Y) is measured with a sensor. Value of measured variable is transferred to the controller. The controller compares obtained (Y) value with desired (Ysp). If both are same then no control action is needed.

If the disturbance (D) affects the process which leads to deviation of the value of the controlled variable (Y) from (Ysp) then the controller will manipulate the variable U.

The variables Y and U are related to each other; a variation in U causes a variation in Y.

The greatest advantage of this control system is that it does not require any knowledge about the nature of the disturbance (D) or physical model of the process.

The drawback is that it starts corrective action after Deviation affects Process

Process control terms

Open control loop
An open control loop exists where a process variable is measured, but the action is taken not in response to the feedback on the condition of the process variable.

Feedback control loop


A sensor is used to measure the disturbance (D) instead of the controlled variable (Y). The information of this measured disturbance (D) is sent to the controller.

If the disturbance (D) affects the process which leads to deviation of the value of the controlled variable (Y) from (Ysp) then the controller will manipulate the variable U in such a way so as to prevent the effect which may be caused by the disturbance (D) on the process.

Feedback control loop

Advantage:
 - This control system starts to take corrective action before the disturbance has any effect on the operation of the process.

Disadvantage

Knowledge about the Physical model of the process is very important because this information is utilized by the controller in order to manipulate the variable U.
** Often exact models are not available hence approximate models are used to determine what is the relationship between controlled variable (Y), manipulated variable (U) and Disturbance (D).

Another disadvantage is that it is very difficult to account for all the disturbances in the system.
** Some are qualitatively known but it is impossible or very difficult to measure them.

Process control terms


Error
 Is the difference between the measured variable and the setpoint.

Any error is having three major components:
- Magnitude
- Duration
- Rate of change

A. Magnitude
 Is the deviation between the value of setpoint and the process variable.

B. Duration
 Refers to the long of the time that an error condition has existed.

C. Rate of change
 The Slope of the error plot.

D. Offset
 Is a sustained deviation of the process variable for the set point.

E. Load disturbance

Undesired change in one of the factors that can affect the process variable.

F. Control Algorithm f(e)

Is a mathematical expression of a control function for the relation between the error and final control element.

Control algorithms

Modulating Control
Modulating control is a method of regulating a Process’s variable by continuously adjusting a component's position or setting to maintain a desired state, rather than just turning it completely on or off.

It allows for precise, gradual adjustments of variables like flow rate, temperature, or pressure.

This is achieved by a control system that sends a signal to a device, such as a modulating valve or boiler, to operate at an intermediate position instead of just the fully open or fully closed states.

When controller is designed to provide this mode of control, it must contain two adjustments:
One for the gain (Kc) term and one for the manual (m) term.

Proportional control rarely ever keeps the process variable at the set point if there is a change in the set point or disturbances to the process.

The larger Kc is the smaller offset ; however, Kc can’t be increased to infinity because the control loop will become unstable.


The question then is on what basis should the manual reset be automated?
The most common practice is to move the control valve at a speed or rate proportional to the error.
In that it would seem to move the valve faster as the error got larger.

The added control mode is called Integral or reset action.

Proportional – Derivative control

One problem with PI controller bears mentioning. If a control loop using PI control, the possibility exists with the integral mode that the controller will continue to integrate and change the output even outside the operating range of the controller. This condition is called “reset windup”

It is possible to prevent this problem of controller reset windup by using a controller operational feature that limits the integration and the controller output.
This feature is called “anti-reset windup”

The larger Kc is the smaller offset ; however, Kc can’t be increased to infinity because the control loop will become unstable.

We can add to the proportional control another action called derivative action.
This control function produces a corrective action that is proportional to the rate of change of error.
Noting that this additional correction exists only when the error is changing as it disappears when the error stops changing even though there may still be a large error.

Proportional – Integral – Derivative control

The PID controller is used on process that respond slowly and have long periods.

Temperature control is a common example of PID control because the heat rate may have to change rapidly when the temperature measurement begins to change, and the derivate action shortens the response of the slow process to an upset (disturbance).

Modulating Control - Feedback control

A feedback loop measures a process variable and sends the measurement to a controller for comparison to setpoint. If the process variable is not at setpoint, control action is taken to return the process variable to setpoint. The below Figure illustrates a feedback loop in which a transmitter measures the temperature of a fluid and, if necessary, opens or closes a hot steam valve to adjust the fluid’s temperature.

The advantage of a feedback loop is that it directly controls the desired process variable. The disadvantage to feedback loops is that the process variable must leave setpoint for action to be taken.

Modulating Control – Cascade control

Cascade control involves the use of two controllers with the output of the first controller (Primary/ Outer) providing the setpoint for the second controller (Secondary/ Inner), the feedback loop for one controller nestling inside the other. Such a system can give improved response to disturbances. Cascade control is also called master slave control.

Modulating Control - Feedforwad control

Cascade control can reduce the effect of disturbances occurring early in the forward loop, but generally cannot deal with load/demand disturbances which occur close to, or affect directly the process variable as there is no intermediate variable or accessible control point.

In a feed-forward system, the control variable adjustment is not error-based. Instead, it is based on knowledge about the process in the form of a mathematical model of the process and knowledge about, or measurements of, the process disturbances.

Modulating Control – Ratio control

A ratio controller is a special type of feedforward control where disturbances are measured and their ratio is held at a desired set point by controlling one of the streams.

Ratio control can be used when two fluids must be mixed in a specific ratio.  A practical way to do this is to use a standard control system to control the flow on one line.  The same transmitter signal is used as a set point for a second controller which controls the flow in a second line. 

The ratio of one flow rate to the other can be changed by adjusting the gain (or proportional band) of the secondary controller.

Modulating Control – Split-Range control

In a split-range control loop, the controller output is split and sent to two or more control valves. The splitter defines how each valve is sequenced as the controller output changes from 0 to 100%.

In most split-range applications, the controller adjusts the opening of one of the valves when its output is in the range of 0 to 50% and the other valve when its output is in the range of 50% to 100%

Modulating Control - Split-Range control

PIC-01 controls the pressure of the separator for liquid-vapour hydrocarbons, by means of a split-range controller with the output signal divided and sent to two pressure control valves PV-A and PV-B. When the pressure increases, the fluid must be discharged to burn. When the pressure decreases, combustible gas is introduced to compensate for the pressure of the separator.

Modulating Control – Split-Range control

The fuel gas valve (PV-B) must close in response to the increased pressure in the separator, while the flared valve (PV-A) should open when the pressure rises beyond the set point.

- When the pressure increases beyond the set point in the 0-50% range of the controller output, PV-B will close from fully open to fully closed.
- When the pressure increases beyond the set point in the 50-100% range of the controller output, PV-A will open from completely close to fully open.

Another use case of the split range control loop is when a control valve cannot be suitably designed to cover the full operating range of the controller. In that case, the valve with a smaller Cv operated between a range of 0-50% and the other operated for a range of 50-100%
